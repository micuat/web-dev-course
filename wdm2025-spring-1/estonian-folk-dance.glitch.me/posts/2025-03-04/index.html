Estonian Folk Costumes

The clothing worn at the Estonian Dance Festival is an essential part of the event, as it showcases Estonia’s rich folk traditions. Dancers wear traditional Estonian folk costumes (rahvariided), which vary by region and are deeply symbolic. These outfits are often handmade and passed down through generations, making them an important part of Estonian heritage.

Elements of Traditional Estonian Folk Costumes

Each region in Estonia has its own distinct style of folk dress, but there are common elements that appear across different areas:

For Women:

  • Aprons – Sometimes worn over the skirt, often decorated with lace or embroidery. Belts – Woven belts with intricate patterns, sometimes used to indicate marital status.
  • Headwear – Unmarried girls wear ribbons or floral crowns. Married women wear caps or bonnets (tanud), which are often embroidered and lace-trimmed.
  • Jewelry – Large, decorative silver brooches (sõlg) are a signature feature, used to fasten the blouse or shawl.

For Men:

  • Vests & Jackets – Wool vests or long coats, sometimes brightly colored or decorated.
  • Hats – Young men often wear simple wool caps. Older men may wear brimmed felt hats or fur hats in colder months.
  • Footwear – Traditional leather shoes (pastlad), fastened with crisscrossing straps, or knee-high boots.

Regional Differences

  • Setomaa (South-East Estonia) – Women's dresses feature elaborate silver jewelry, large brooches, and richly embroidered aprons.
  • Saaremaa & Hiiumaa (West Estonian islands) – Men's costumes often include striped trousers, and women wear plaid skirts with bold colors.
  • Võrumaa & Mulgimaa (Southern Estonia) – Known for darker, more subdued colors and elegant shawls.
